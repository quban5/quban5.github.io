Warsaw Merchants

Board Game | Digital Game

Description

Warsaw Merchants is an economic board game for 2–4 players. Players aim to buy goods at the lowest price and sell them at the highest price possible. Players must adapt to changing demand and prices and choose their strategies accordingly, while sometimes relying on a bit of luck.

I was inspired by Bolesław Prus’s novel The Doll. I liked the concept of competing 19th-century shops trading various goods. The game’s title and visual style reference Warsaw merchants, and the currency is rubles.


Game Elements

  • Goods table
  • Goods – jewelry, coffee, spices, tobacco, textiles, toys
  • Demand cards for goods (matching the goods list)
  • Dice – one white and one black
  • Money
  • Bonds
  • Event cards
  • Warehouses
  • Priority token
  • Time pawn

Setup

  1. Allocate starting money to each player (depending on player count).
  2. Assign warehouses to players.
  3. Shuffle and deal three demand cards for January, February, and March.
  4. Shuffle the goods deck and place it on the goods table.
  5. Place the time pawn on the January space.

How to Play?

1. Roll dice and update goods table

At the start of each month, the player with the priority token rolls both dice. The number on the black die indicates how many positions to move a specific good up or down on the table. A question mark symbol means drawing the top event card and placing it in its designated spot on the board (it does not affect the goods table).

The number on the white die indicates the number of customers (explained below).

EXAMPLE – Using the black die
Suppose jewelry was assigned to January and is in the 3rd position on the goods table. If the black die shows +2, move jewelry to the 1st position (3 – 2 = 1).

2. Players buy goods from the market

At the start of each quarter (marked in green on the board), players enter the buying phase. They can buy goods, bonds, and warehouse upgrades using their money.

The player with the priority token begins (usually the youngest or least experienced). Purchase order continues clockwise.

Check the goods table for base prices. A good’s position indicates its demand multiplier. Calculate the current price by multiplying or dividing the base price by the indicated factor.

Players can only buy what fits in their warehouse. The default capacity is 20 units. Spent money goes to the bank.

EXAMPLE – Buying goods
A player wants to buy toys, which are in 5th position. Base price is 4 rubles with a /2 divider. Current price is 4 / 2 = 2 rubles. The player buys 20 units (their warehouse limit) for a total of 40 rubles.


3. Selling goods

Each month, players may sell goods. Selling price depends on table position, and customer count depends on the white die roll multiplied by the demand multiplier.

Players must sell at least one type of good per month but can choose how many units of each.

EXAMPLE – Selling goods
Player 1 has 20 toys and 30 tobacco. White die roll is 2. Toys (5th position, x1 multiplier): at least 2 customers. Tobacco (3rd position, x2 multiplier): at least 4 customers. Player sells all toys at 2 rubles each (40 rubles total) and sells 4 tobacco at 8 rubles each (32 rubles).


4. Advance to next month

After selling, move the time pawn to the next month and repeat rolls and actions. Buying only occurs at the start of each quarter. The priority token passes clockwise at the start of a new quarter.


5. Warehouse upgrades

Warehouse capacity starts at 20 and can be increased during buying phases. Exact upgrade costs and capacities are TBD and require playtesting for balance.


6. Events

Two faces of the black die show question marks. When rolled, draw an event card, read it, and place it in the event area. Events add variety by introducing bonuses or penalties.

EXAMPLE – Event cards
Aristocrats' Ball (October): Increase demand: Jewelry +1, Textiles +2. Apply after dice but before sales.

Trade Route War: At the start of the next quarter, coffee and spices cannot be purchased.


7. Bonds

During buying phases, players may purchase bonds. After the bond’s term, players receive their investment plus interest. Available terms are one year and multi-year; multi-year bonds yield higher returns.


8. Turn sequence

  1. Buy goods, bonds, upgrades
  2. Roll dice
  3. Update goods table (dice first, then events)
  4. Sell goods
  5. Advance time pawn

9. Win Condition

The player with the highest net worth after a set number of years (e.g., 10) wins. Net worth includes cash, bond value at current interest, upgraded warehouse value, and goods valued at current market prices.


10. Challenges and Considerations

  • Balance concerns: Prices, multipliers, and quantities need extensive playtesting with multiple players.
  • Players with too much cash could corner a good’s supply; limited tokens may mitigate this.
  • Physical game bookkeeping (tokens, money) can be tedious; a digital version could automate these processes.
  • The game might feel smoother and more accessible as a digital-only experience.
